Case Study: The Dublin Mint Office Limited.

Summary of the Case study: A complainant reported to the DPC On 13 October 2027 about him receiving two telephone call the same day from the Dublin Mint Office Limited. The complainant further explained that the caller has attempted to sell some commemorative coin, which he had registered online a few months earlier with the company for an online offer on behalf of him and his son.

Moreover, the complainant emphasize that while going through the registration process, where telephone contact was required he had registered and included the same contact number for himself and his son during the registration process. However, he stated that he had ticked the marketing opt-out box during the online registration process.

What is the specific aspect of GDPR that your case study addresses?

This case study addresses the aspect of an unsolicited marketing communications by the company. Stated in the GDPR document according to article 4(2) of the GDPR (2017), If your organism acts both as a processor and controller, the record must clearly distinguish the two categories of activities. However, in the case of Dublin Mint Office the data entry did not clearly distinguish between mails and emails marketing and telemarketing.

While investigation is being carried out by the DPC, the Dublin Mint Office Limited admitted that it had made the marketing telephone calls. It explained that when the complainant supplied his telephone number during the online application process the order form had only offered an opt-in option to receive marketing mails and emails. Furthermore, the company explained that the complainant had not selected the opt-in option that the cause was as a result of the gap in the system in place at the time the complainant registered only allowed for an opt-in to marketing mails and emails but not an opt-out for telesales.

How was it resolved?

Warning was issued to the Dublin Mint Office Limited in September 2017 by the DPC concerning other complaints which had been made, the DPC therefore decided to prosecute the Dublin Mint Office Limited as the company pleaded guilty to two charges in relation to both marketing telephone calls and also agreed to cover the DPC's prosecution costs in lieu of a conviction and fine the court applied section 1(1) of the probation of offenders Act.

If this was your organization, what steps would you take as an Information Security Manager to mitigate the issue?

As an information security manager I'll make sure;

A. There's provision of sensitization programs for members to create their awareness to collecting personal data, verifying this data before acting upon it and the penalty for violating the GDPR.

B That consistent and proper maintenance is being carried out on the system in use and also make sure that the behavior of the system is being monitored well as it evolves overtime in order to avoid collection of wrong information.

If it is the case that the company intentionally does not differentiate the user data into multiple groups for different activities, the requirement of Article 4(2) of the GDPR (2018) has been violated, as you have pointed out.
