Initial Post :- Collaborative Discussion 1: Project Failures Study

What do you believe are the three most common reasons for project failure?.

1. Poor communication and incompetent project manager.

2. Inadequate organization support and task definitions.

3. Lack of adequate knowledge.

“Give two examples of failures that support your choices (there are several examples in the lecturecast).”

An important case study is the issue of a medium-sized international software product company. The company releases new software versions regularly and its products can be characterized as complex model-based software. The failure of the case was that blocker-type defects of the program are often detected after the product releases (Lehtinen et al, 2014). This project's failure is due to the lack of adequate skills required and knowledge in testing and having code quality control in the case of adding new codes to the old ones. Developers and testers should identify this common error during the development and testing phase, but they didn't.

Another Case Study is a medium-sized international software product company with approximately 100 employees. The product is delivered for the customers through installation projects that occasionally include the development of new features. New software versions are released regularly. The failure selected for analysis in the case was that the installation projects are too challenging to be performed efficiently. This project failure was due to lack of communication and incompetent project managers who has failed to develop a method of creating checklists and simplifying the installation process which could have minimize the failure.

Collaborative Discussion 1: Response to Doug Millward

In my opinion, I believe it can help. Automated testing is relatively a new approach to software testing which guarantee and ensures quality test at all times. Automated testing is a very critical model of continuous testing which ensures continuous testing of the software components at all time when the need arises. With automated testing, rather than performing a big bang critical testing at the end of the software development life cycle it happens in small pieces (Step by step) when the need for testing arises.

Concerning the second case study, in my opinion it does. Advance persistent threat are not random threat because it requires excessive resources than a standard web application attack. Advance Persistent Attacks are being initiated by team of competent and experienced cybercriminals with enough and substantial financial banking. Most of which are government funded used as cyber warfare weapons. Those attackers usually establish an illicit, long-term presence on a network in order to mine highly sensitive data. The target of these attacks are usually carefully chosen, researched and assessed. With these kind of attacks, I concur to the opinion that such attacks are not responsible for software failure.

Collaborative Discussion 1: Project Failures Study: By Ian Wollof

Interesting post especially the section about code quality which is an area I know quite well. while no software is defect free according to Carnegie Mellon University's Sustainable Computing Consortium “Commercial software typically has 20 to 30 bugs for every 1,000 lines of code” (Wired Magazine Staff) it is also the case that software development cycles have shrunk with the advent of modern tooling and increased use of micro services. It used to be common for a major software release every 24 months this moved to yearly releases and now with the evergreen model (Anon) And the increased use of automated CI/DI pipelines, code may not have the level of oversight it once had before being packaged and productised.

Software is also now orders of magnitude more complex than in the early days of software development. (Anon) So, we have increased complexity and smaller development cycles resulting in potentially more software defects making it into production code. However to counter this trend the technology and science of software development has also matured rapidly and the testing and SSAST tools now in use such as SonarQube , code climate etc to capture these errors are also more advance and with the new emerging technologies of machine learning now being used in the field of code analytics the future for development of high quality software is looking positive.

Tooling can only take us so far and the most important factor in delivering a product / project is still having the right people with the right skills working on the project, this can make or break a project. This is just as true now as it’s always been. From the Romans in 312bc (National Geographic 2022) building viaducts to modern Advance AI coding you need to ensure the correct skills are present in the team to successfully deliver the desired outcome.
