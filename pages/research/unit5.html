Unit 5 - interviews | survey

Interviews and survey methods in research. In-depth interviews are a common qualitative research method that involves conversing with a single respondent to gather detailed information. Surveys, on the other hand, are a quantitative method that involves collecting data from a larger group of respondents. These methods are often used together to gain a more comprehensive understanding of a research topic. Pre- and post-testing methods, which involve collecting data before and after the implementation of a new process or system, can also be used in conjunction with these research methods to provide a before-and-after picture of the change. Overall, week 5 introduced the use of interviews and surveys in research and discussed the importance of using these methods effectively

Case Study: Inappropriate Use of Surveys

In 2018, Cambridge Analytica was in the news in the United Kingdom and the USA (Confessore, 2018) for obtaining and sharing data obtained from millions of Facebook users. They obtained the data through innocuous surveys on Facebook (you may have seen this type of survey and probably participated at times). This is probably the highest profile of surveys used for alternative means and, probably, monetary gains. However, this happens often through various media. Consider how exactly this happened and why it was used. Find one or two further examples of inappropriate use of surveys and highlight the impact of all these examples from the various ethical, social, legal and professional standpoints that apply. Record your findings in your e-Portfolio. You can also submit your findings to your tutor for formative feedback.

The case of the '' Data harvesting" by the Cambridge Analytica was major issues about the unethical use of surveys to elicit data for respondents with obtaining appropriate consent.

This was done through the online surveys distributed to a wide range of internet users around the world via the social media platform called Facebook. Over 50 million users were involved in this scandal as the Cambridge Analytica had been eliciting information from the social media application users for the purpose of predicting and influencing politics online.

The activity of Cambridge Analytica is marketed on its official website as a blend of predictive analytics, behavioral science, and data-driven advertising technology. This combination aims to create a target audience and utilize the information to influence and inspire the voters (Boldyreva et al., 2018).

The Cambridge Analytica was charged to court for carrying out illegal use of surveys through the use of the internet. The ethical issue in this case study is the inadequate regulation of private user data. The participants in the survey were not aware of the use of the survey for political or commercial purposes though the company committed an ethical offense of deception, disregarding consent as a research ethics.

Another example of the inappropriate use of surveys is made possible through data mining. Quinn, (2009) illustrated how sometimes customers get a popup requesting they fill out an application that asks for a lot of personal information, such as name, address, and phone number. The online store also enters information about your purchases into a database.

A database record keeps track of a single transaction, like the specific supermarket item you bought. An individual's history of purchases are captured in a single database record. Although it offers some insight into the individual, its use when considered alone is constrained. Data mining, on the other hand, is the act of looking for patterns or links among numerous entries in one or more databases.

By merging information from several transactions, data mining may create new knowledge and make predictions about the future. Data mining enables an organization to create a precise profile of an individual from a variety of snapshots by drawing from a vast number of information. Companies are leveraging data mining to forge more intimate connections with their customers, as seen by Google's customized search and collaborative filtering.

Boldyreva, E.L., Duisembina, E. and Grishina, N., 2018, December. Cambridge analytica: ethics and online manipulation with decision-making process. https://dx.doi.org/10.15405/epsbs.2018.12.02.10

Quinn, M.J., 2009. Ethics for the information age (pp. 137-138). Boston: Pearson Education.
