Unit 4 - Case Study | Observation

Case studies involve in-depth research and study of individuals or groups. This leads to a hypothesis and widens the scope of studying a phenomenon. However, case studies should not be used to determine cause and effect because they cannot make accurate predictions and there could be an atypical respondent in the study. A focus group is another commonly used qualitative research method where a group of people is selected and allowed to express their insights on the topic being studied. The individuals in the focus group should have a common background, comparable experiences, and be representative of the target market. The aim of the focus group is to find answers to the "why", "what", and "how" questions. Quantitative observation involves observing numerical values, such as weight, volume, or shape, from a sample that represents the target market or goal. The data is collected objectively and then analyzed using statistical and numerical methods to obtain research results. Qualitative observation involves monitoring characteristics by observing predetermined respondents from a distance. In this data gathering method, the respondents should be in a comfortable environment to ensure that the characteristics observed are natural and effective. In a descriptive research design, the researcher can choose to be a complete observer, an observer as a participant, a participant as an observer, or a full participant.

Seminar Three: Case study - Privacy

Please read the case below and answer the questions. You need to determine the ethical issues involved with this case. Ricardo works for the records department of his local government as a computer records clerk, where he has access to files of property tax records. For a scientific study, a researcher, Beth, has been granted access to the numerical portion "but not the corresponding names" of some records. Beth finds some information that she would like to use, but she needs the names and addresses corresponding with certain properties. Beth asks Ricardo to retrieve these names and addresses, so she can contact these people for more information and for permission to do further study. Now consider, what are the ethical issues involved in deciding which of these options to pursue?

Introduction

Ethics is an application of generally accepted social norms (morals) in governing behavior – that is, they help one establish right action from wrong (Rauh, J., 2016). Research ethics majorly revolve around two blocks; the first block involves ethical issues of consent, privacy, harm to participants, and the confidentiality of shared data. The second block deals with professional ethics like honesty, integrity, and the responsible reporting of the data. The first group deals with the treatment of data and participants while the second describes the best fit ethical behaviors for researchers.

• If Ricardo is not responsible for determining allowable access, should he release the names and addresses?

No, If Ricardo is not responsible for determining allowable access, He may not release the names and addresses of tax payers to the researcher, Beth. This is because this violates the right to confidentiality of tax payers information. If Ricardo as Tax preparer releases such information to another personnel (in his case, Beth a researcher) he will face a penalty of criminal fines or prison. Beth will have to apply to the appropriate authorities to obtain permission to collect the personal information that she needs for her research or to contact the taxpayers for further research.

• Suppose Ricardo were responsible for determining allowable access to the files, what ethical issues would be involved in his deciding whether to grant access to Beth?

In the case where Ricardo is responsible for determining allowable access to the files, the individuals whose information are to released have to be contacted by the records department to seek their consent and willing participation in the research and the permission to release their personal information.This is known as Informed Consent. Also the researcher must be enlightened on several requirements for release of the names of the tax payers to the public are on several grounds;
1) The researcher may not use the information in any court case.
2) Data must be de-identified before release to the researcher ( in this case we ignore this as the identification of data was specified but researcher may not expose their personal information to the general public.
3) Consent of study subjects can be reasonably presumed.
4) Outcomes of the analysis must not allow re-identifying participants.
5) Use of the data must not result in any damage or distress.

• Should Beth be allowed to contact the individuals involved? That is, should the Records department release individuals' names to a researcher? What are the ethical issues for the Records department to consider?

The Records Department must bear in mind the following ethics. when deciding whether to release individual names to the researcher
1) Informed Consent and Beneficence: the individuals whose information are to be released have to be informed of the release of their personal information for research purposes, appropriate measures have to be taken to ensure that they are protected from unforseen harm that may result from the release of this private information.
2) Respect for anonymity and confidentiality : If the participants are willing to volunteer to share private or sensitive information, the researchers must ensure extra efforts to keep the information confidential. Insensitive data collection methods might hurt the research participants’ privacy.
3) Informed Consent and Respect for Privacy: Informal and in-depth interviews can lead to greater exposure to ethical standards. Potential ethical issues may arise from the types and quality of data that needs to be obtained, the procedure of storing the data, and how to inform the participants that their data will be used (Rana et al., 2021).

• Suppose Beth contacts the individuals to ask their permission, and one-third of them respond giving permission, one-third respond denying permission, and one-third do not respond. Beth claims that at least one-half of the individuals are needed to make a valid study. What options are available to Beth?

Beth as a researcher has several options to consider:
1) Give up on the research
2) Wait for further response from the one third that did not respond or resend her request for their permissions.
3) Commit Academic fraud or misinformation. Some researchers have carried out research misconduct in cases of academic fraud. They submit research works for publications that contain falsified information. This publication will lack academic integrity, are usually highly plagiarized or contain claims that cannot be verified. Such works are dangerous to future researchers as they will base their own work on the shoulders of already existing " trustworthy" research. Hence if one- half of the Individuals are needed for a valid research and Beth goes ahead to use one- third of the Individuals, she produces an invalid research work for the public.
